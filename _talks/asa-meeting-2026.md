## Abstract
Empirical research on antisemitism in Germany has long emphasized its multidimensional character, distinguishing between classical antisemitism, so-called secondary antisemitism characterized by perpetrator–victim reversal, and more recent forms such as the projection of anti-Jewish resentments onto the State of Israel. Long-term population surveys indicate that particularly classical antisemitism remains prevalent and functions as a reservoir from which antisemitic prejudice is rearticulated in response to changing political and social contexts. This dynamic becomes especially pronounced during periods when the Israeli–Palestinian conflict moves to the center of media coverage, political debate, and public discourse. In the aftermath of the Hamas pogrom of October 7, 2023, and the subsequent war in Gaza, both conceptual and empirical distinctions between classical and Israel-related antisemitism have been increasingly contested, alongside intensified debates over when political criticism of Israeli state policies crosses the line into antisemitic rhetoric. At the same time, public and academic attention has increasingly focused on the prevalence of antisemitism among individuals with a Muslim background and among migrants from countries with historically or politically entrenched antisemitic traditions, despite the continued scarcity of systematic large-scale survey research. In our talk, we aim to address this research gap by presenting findings from joint work examining the nexus of religion, migration, political orientation, and antisemitism in Germany. Drawing on survey data collected before and after 2023 and including sufficiently large samples of societal minority groups, we investigate four core questions: (1) how classical and contemporary forms of antisemitism are measured and empirically related to each other and to non-antisemitic criticism of Israeli policies; (2) how religious affiliation and migration background are associated with different manifestations of antisemitism; (3) which established and emerging predictors, including political ideology, social dominance orientation, right-wing authoritarianism, conspiracy mentality, and religious fundamentalism, account for antisemitic attitudes when religious and migratory factors are considered; and (4) which patterns characterize support for Hamas and the glorification of political violence.
